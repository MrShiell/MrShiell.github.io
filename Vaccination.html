Vaccines are typically a dead or altered form of a disease-causing pathogen, which contain a specific antigen, to be introduced into the body. This causes the immune system, specifically the white blood cells, to produce complementary antibodies. The antibodies target the antigen and attach themselves to it in order to create memory cells. Since the pathogen is inactivated, with vaccination this primary immune response is complete in the absence of the disease. This allows our body to quickly respond to the antigen if it is encountered again preventing illness in the individual.

How Vaccines work (Answers to page 6 of your booklet)

    • 1) The antigens of a disease are inserted into the body.
      2) These can be a "non-active" form of the disease or the antigen, or parts of the antigen.
      3) The lymphocytes recognise that there is a foreign antigen in the body
      4) This triggers an immune response
      5) Lymphocyteswork out and make the correct antibodies to help destroy the antigens
      6) If the body comes across the same antigen again the correct antubodies can be made very quickly because memory cells remain in the body.
      7) Memory cells remember which antibody works for each antigen (this is a very specific response
      8) These memory cells therefore provide immunity, after exposure to a vaccine or after a natural infection
