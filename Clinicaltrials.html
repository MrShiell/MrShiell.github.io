Drug discovery

New drugs need to be tested before they come on to the wider market for a variety of reasons including:

  • - Testing for efficacy (does it work)
  • - Testing for dosage (at what dose does it work)
  • - Toxicity (at what dose is it toxic
  • - What side effects can occur
  • - Are there any interactions with any medications?

A new drug or treatment is first researched in a lab typically within cell cultures or even computer simulations. Here researchers study the efficacy of a treatment and test the effects of various dosages. If successful researchers will apply for funding for animal trials. Additionally, researchers typically must pass an ethics committee before starting any animal testing.

Human clinical trials

If animal testing is successful then researchers move on to human clinical trials. Typically this is segmented into 4 phases. Phase 1 represents the last pre-clinical trial and is comprised of a small group of healthy volunteers given the drug/treatment at a small dosage. As with all human trials one group is given a placebo while the other is given the treatment.

Placebo: An inactive substance made to resemble a drug for researchers to use as a control

If successful the researchers move on to phase 2&3 clinical trials where the drug is tested on people with the illness. Researchers again monitor for any potential side effects of interactions.
